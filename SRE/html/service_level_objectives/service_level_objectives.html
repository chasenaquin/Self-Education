  • It is impossible to manage a service correctly, let alone well, without understanding which behaviors really matter for that service and how to measure and evaluate those behaviors.
  • We use intuition, experience, and understanding of what users want to define Service Level Indicators (SLIs), Objectives (SLOs), and Agreements (SLAs).
  • These measurements describe the basic properties of metrics that matter, what values we want those metrics to have, and how we will react if we can’t provide the expected service.
  • Metric Modeling, Metric Selection, and Metric Analysis

Service Level Terminology

  • Indicators
    • A carefully defined quantitative measure of some aspect of the level of service that is provided.
      • Request latency (How long it takes to return a response to a request)
      • Error rate (a fraction of all requests received)
      • System Throughput (requests per second)
      • Availability (fraction of the time that the service is usable) (Yield)
      • Durability (The likelihood that data will be retained over a long period of time
    • Ideally, the SLI directly measures a service level of interest, but sometimes only a proxy is available
  • Objectives
    • A target value or range of values for a service level that is measured by an SLI.
    • SLI <= Target or Lower Bound <= SLI <= Upper Bound.
    • Higher QPS often leads to larger latency, and its common for services to have a performance cliff beyond some load threshold.
    • Publishing the SLOs to users set correct expectations.
  • Agreements
    • An explicit or implicit contract with your users that includes the consequences of meeting (or missing) the SLO they contain.  (What happens if the SLO's are not met?)
    • Most recognizable when they are financial (rebate / penalty).
    • SRE not typically involved in making SLA's (closely tied to business and product decisions)

The Global Chubby Planned Outage

  • A controlled outage synthesized by intentionally taking down the system resulted in flushing out unreasonable dependencies on the service.

Indicators in Practice

  • What do you and your users care about?
  • Chose a handful of representative indicators that show system health.
  • Services tend to fall into a few braod categories in terms of the SLIs they find relevant:
    • User-facing systems
      • Availability (Could we respond to the request)
      • Latency (How long did it take to respond)
      • Throughput (How many requests could be handled)
